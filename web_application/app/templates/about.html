About Us

Our Vision

This project was funded by the MSU Institute of Water Research with the goal of addressing critical challenges in hydrological modeling and water resource management in Michigan. Our focus is to develop a scalable, physically informed deep learning model that predicts high-resolution hydrological variables, especially groundwater recharge, with applications across various watersheds in the United States.

The Problem

Estimating groundwater recharge is a computationally intensive and complex task, requiring significant resources for calibration and simulation using physically based models. These challenges become more pronounced when expanding to high-resolution spatiotemporal predictions over large geographic areas. Moreover, the impact of climate change and human activities on groundwater systems demands innovative approaches to better understand and manage these vital resources.

Our Approach

We propose a hybrid framework that combines the strengths of physically based hydrological models like SWAT+ with cutting-edge deep learning techniques. By leveraging a CNN-Transformer architecture, our system learns from SWAT+ model outputs and national climatological datasets to predict groundwater recharge for regions lacking direct monitoring or hydrological models.

  • Integration of SWAT+ simulations with high-resolution datasets such as PRISM, LOCA2, NSRDB, and gSSURGO.
  • Development of a novel CNN-Transformer model with 128 million parameters for spatiotemporal regression tasks.
  • Deployment of scalable workflows to predict hydrological variables using only publicly available data, enabling collaborative research and model reuse.

Key Objectives

  1. Provide a scalable framework for hydrological data modeling across the United States.
  2. Develop a deep learning architecture capable of high-resolution, spatiotemporal predictions.
  3. Facilitate collaboration by creating a platform for researchers to access data and models.

Collaboration and Accessibility

Our system is designed to foster collaboration by serving as a centralized environment where researchers can contribute and utilize data for hydrological studies. The pre-trained CNN-Transformer model is built to operate independently of SWAT+ models, making it highly accessible for diverse applications.
