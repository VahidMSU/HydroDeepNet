Welcome to the Physically Informed Deep Learning Platform

Overview

The Physically Informed Deep Learning Platform integrates hydrological modeling, hierarchical data management, and advanced deep learning techniques to predict hydrological variables at high spatial and temporal resolution. This system is designed to enhance our understanding of surface and subsurface hydrological processes, with a focus on groundwater recharge, evapotranspiration, and surface runoff.

The platform combines three key components: (1) physically based hydrological modeling using SWAT+, (2) hierarchical data organization and preprocessing, and (3) a custom CNN-Transformer architecture for spatiotemporal regression. Each stage builds on the other, ensuring accuracy, scalability, and adaptability for various hydrological applications.

Key Components

1. Hydrological Modeling with SWAT+

SWAT+ (Soil and Water Assessment Tool Plus) serves as the foundation for generating hydrological outputs. Using the NHDPlus High Resolution (HR) dataset, SWAT+ simulates surface and subsurface hydrological cycles across multiple watersheds. Key aspects include:

  • Representation of hydrological processes like evapotranspiration, runoff, and groundwater recharge.
  • Use of hierarchical land classifications, from basins to hydrological response units (HRUs).
  • Calibration of model parameters using Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) to ensure accuracy in predicting observed data.

2. Hierarchical Data Management

Managing and preprocessing large datasets is crucial for effective modeling and analysis. The platform employs a hierarchical database system to integrate and standardize data from multiple sources:

  • High-resolution datasets, including PRISM (4km), MODIS (250m), NLCD (30m), and gSSURGO (30m).
  • Consistent spatial and temporal preprocessing to align data at a 250m resolution across Michigan's Lower Peninsula.
  • Gap-filling techniques to handle missing values in remote sensing data, ensuring temporal continuity and reliability.

3. Deep Learning with CNN-Transformer Models

At the core of the platform lies a custom CNN-Transformer architecture designed for spatiotemporal regression tasks. This model leverages convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for spatial feature extraction and Transformers for modeling temporal dependencies. Key features include:

  • Processing multi-channel spatiotemporal data in tensor format [batch_size, time_steps, channels, height, width].
  • Down-sampling and up-sampling pathways with skip connections to preserve spatial details.
  • Use of positional encoding and attention mechanisms to capture temporal patterns effectively.

Methodology

The platform follows a systematic approach to integrate hydrological modeling, data management, and deep learning:

  1. Generate SWAT+ Models: Predefined hydrological models are created using the NHDPlus HR dataset, calibrated with streamflow and other observed data. A total of 58 SWAT+ models are implemented across Michigan’s Lower Peninsula, covering watersheds ranging from 50 km² to 3,500 km².
  2. Preprocess Datasets: Data from multiple sources (MODIS, PRISM, NLCD) is aligned spatially and temporally. Missing values are addressed through interpolation and averaging techniques, ensuring clean inputs for deep learning.
  3. Train CNN-Transformer Models: The deep learning model is trained using SWAT+-simulated outputs (e.g., recharge, runoff) alongside geospatial and remote sensing datasets. The trained model is then used to predict hydrological variables in ungauged watersheds where SWAT+ model is not available.

Applications

The Physically Informed Deep Learning Platform is designed to address key challenges in hydrology and water resource management:

  • Predicting groundwater recharge in areas without observational data.
  • Assessing the impacts of climate change on water availability and hydrological processes.
  • Providing scalable solutions for watershed-level hydrological modeling and analysis.

Educational Value

Beyond its research applications, this platform serves as a valuable tool for education and training in hydrology and machine learning. It demonstrates the integration of physically based models with modern deep learning approaches, bridging the gap between domain knowledge and computational advancements.
